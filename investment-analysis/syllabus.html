Student overview

Six units, thirty taught lessons

Each 40-minute lesson has a company example, one analyst question, a clear knowledge target and a short check. Short one-class team tasks appear when useful; assessment checks stay small and contained.

Before the first share

Start with the price idea

Students first learn what a stock price represents: the market price of one ownership unit at a specific time, not the value of the whole company.

Foundation exercise

Explain why prices move

Students connect new information to expectations, buyers and sellers, then calculate a simple price change using the old price as the base.

Lesson 1Tencent

What does Tencent's stock price represent, and why can it change?

Focus: what one share price represents, why new information can move it, and why price matters before judging an investment.

  • share - one unit of ownership in a company.
  • shareholder - a person or institution that owns one or more shares.
  • share price - the market price of one share at a specific time.
  • market price - a price formed by buyers and sellers in the market.
  • expectations - investors' views about future profit and risk.

Formula: % change = (new price - old price) / old price x 100.

Evidence: Tencent listing checked quickly, source date, revenue, gross profit and gross margin as possible price-changing information.

Check: Explain what a stock price represents, complete the information-expectations-price chain and calculate one simple price change.

Lesson 2HKEX

Why do companies need a stock market?

Focus: market infrastructure, liquidity and why real buying and selling has friction.

  • stock exchange - a regulated market where listed securities can be bought and sold.
  • listing - permission for a company's shares to trade on an exchange.
  • stock code - the short market identifier used to find a listed security.
  • liquidity - how easily an asset can be bought or sold without a large price change.
  • transaction cost - a fee or cost paid when buying or selling.
  • market order - an order to trade quickly at the best available price.
  • limit order - an order to trade only at a chosen price or better.

Formula: no new formula; students identify company, exchange, code, order type and possible cost accurately.

Evidence: HKEX role, exchange website source, example stock codes, order examples and listing context.

Check: Match companies to exchange, code and listing information, then choose whether a simple case needs a market order or limit order.

Lesson 3Alibaba

What can a stock quote tell us, and what is still missing?

Focus: reading quote pages, bid/ask data and why a displayed price is not a promise of instant execution.

  • quote page - a market snapshot showing price and trading information.
  • volume - the number of shares traded during a period.
  • previous close - the last price recorded at the end of the previous trading day.
  • bid - the price buyers are currently willing to pay.
  • ask - the price sellers are currently asking for.
  • bid-ask spread - the gap between the ask price and bid price.

Formula: spread = ask price - bid price; daily change = current price - previous close.

Evidence: Alibaba stock quote screenshot or frozen quote table with bid, ask, volume, date and source.

Check: Label what a quote page proves, calculate the spread and explain what it cannot promise.

Lesson 4Xiaomi

Does owning shares mean you control the company?

Focus: ownership size, voting rights and influence.

  • voting rights - shareholder rights to vote on certain company decisions.
  • ordinary share - a standard ownership share in a company.
  • control - the ability to influence or decide company actions.

Formula: ownership % = shares owned / total shares x 100.

Evidence: Xiaomi shareholder or share-capital extract with source and date.

Check: Compare a tiny shareholder with a large holder and explain who has more influence.

Lesson 5JD.com

How can one analyst collect evidence before judging a share?

Focus: source discipline and individual analyst habits.

  • evidence log - a structured record of sources, dates, figures and notes.
  • source date - the date when evidence was published.
  • accessed date - the date when you used the evidence.

Formula: no new formula; the skill is clean evidence recording.

Evidence: JD.com annual results, investor-relations page and a frozen classroom evidence log.

Check: You complete one individual evidence log row without making a recommendation.

Lesson 6Meituan

Does high revenue mean a company is strong?

Focus: revenue, growth and business model.

  • revenue - income earned from selling goods or services before costs are deducted.
  • sales growth - the increase in revenue over time.
  • business model - how a company earns revenue and delivers value.

Formula: revenue growth = (new revenue - old revenue) / old revenue x 100.

Evidence: Meituan revenue by year or segment, with source and date.

Check: Explain why high revenue alone does not prove high profit.

Lesson 7BYD

Can sales rise while costs become a problem?

Focus: direct costs, gross profit and gross margin.

  • cost of sales - direct costs linked to producing or delivering goods and services.
  • gross profit - revenue left after direct costs are deducted.
  • gross margin - gross profit as a percentage of revenue.

Formula: gross profit = revenue - cost of sales; gross margin = gross profit / revenue x 100.

Evidence: BYD revenue and cost figures from annual or interim results.

Check: Calculate gross profit and explain why margin matters.

Lesson 8CATL

Which company turns sales into profit more efficiently?

Focus: margin comparison and efficiency.

  • profit margin - profit as a percentage of revenue.
  • operating profit - profit from the company's main operations before some finance and tax items.
  • comparison - judging one figure against another company, year or benchmark.

Formula: margin difference = margin A - margin B, measured in percentage points.

Evidence: CATL margin data and one comparison point from the same source type.

Check: You state which business is more efficient and support it with a margin figure.

Lesson 9Tesla

Why can a growing company still need cash?

Focus: cash flow, profit and investment needs.

  • cash flow - the movement of cash into and out of a business.
  • profit - revenue left after costs are deducted.
  • working capital - money tied up in day-to-day operations.

Formula: free cash flow = operating cash flow - capital expenditure.

Evidence: Tesla cash-flow extract and capital expenditure figure from a filed report.

Check: Explain why profit and cash flow can send different signals.

Lesson 10TSMC

What makes one strong company look stronger than another?

Focus: side-by-side comparison across scale, trend and margin.

  • benchmark - a standard or comparison point used to judge performance.
  • trend - movement in a figure over time.
  • scale - the size of a company or activity.

Formula: compare growth, margin and scale side by side; no single new formula.

Evidence: TSMC revenue, margin and trend figures with one relevant benchmark.

Check: Write one comparison sentence using a figure from each company.

Lesson 11Apple

How does a share make or lose money for an investor?

Focus: capital gains, losses and percentage return.

  • capital gain - profit from selling a share above the purchase price.
  • capital loss - loss from selling a share below the purchase price.
  • return - gain or loss compared with the original amount invested.

Formula: gain = sale price - purchase price; return % = gain / purchase price x 100.

Evidence: Apple frozen share-price example with dates, not live trading data.

Check: Calculate a gain, a loss and the percentage return.

Lesson 12HSBC

Why might an investor like dividends?

Focus: income return and total return.

  • dividend - profit paid by a company to shareholders.
  • dividend yield - annual dividend compared with the share price.
  • total return - return from price change plus dividends.

Formula: dividend yield = annual dividend / share price x 100; total return = capital gain + dividends.

Evidence: HSBC dividend announcement or annual report extract with date.

Check: Explain why two investors may value the same dividend differently.

Lesson 13Nvidia

How can one company be worth so much?

Focus: market capitalisation and company scale.

  • market capitalisation - total market value of a company's equity.
  • shares outstanding - shares issued and held by investors.
  • mega-cap - a very large listed company by market value.

Formula: market capitalisation = share price x shares outstanding.

Evidence: Nvidia share price and shares outstanding from a frozen source.

Check: Calculate a simplified market cap and explain what it does not prove.

Lesson 14Microsoft

When do investors pay a high price for profit?

Focus: EPS, P/E and expectations.

  • EPS - earnings per share, or net profit divided by shares.
  • P/E ratio - share price compared with earnings per share.
  • valuation - judgement of price compared with evidence and expectations.

Formula: EPS = net profit / shares; P/E = share price / EPS.

Evidence: Microsoft EPS and price example, frozen with source and date.

Check: Explain why a high P/E may show optimism, risk or both.

Lesson 15Toyota

Can a good company still be too expensive?

Focus: valuation checkpoint using several pieces of evidence.

  • cheap or expensive - valuation judgement, not just a low or high share price.
  • expectations - assumptions about future performance already reflected in price.
  • margin of safety - room for error between price and estimated value.

Formula: compare P/E, growth and risk evidence; no new calculation.

Evidence: Toyota growth, P/E or profitability snapshot with a valuation prompt.

Check: Write a two-point answer explaining why quality and price must be judged together.

Lesson 16Starbucks

What could hurt one familiar company?

Focus: company-specific risk.

  • company-specific risk - risk caused by one company's decisions or situation.
  • demand risk - the risk that customers buy less than expected.
  • execution risk - the risk that a strategy is not carried out successfully.

Formula: no new formula; build a risk-evidence-effect chain.

Evidence: Starbucks store, demand or margin evidence from a recent report.

Check: Name one risk and link it to future revenue or profit.

Lesson 17Li Ning

How can changing tastes affect a sportswear company?

Focus: sector risk and competition.

  • sector - a group of companies selling similar goods or services.
  • competition - rivalry between firms for customers and profit.
  • consumer trend - a change in what customers prefer to buy.

Formula: market share = company sales / sector sales x 100, if suitable data is available.

Evidence: Li Ning sales trend, competitor context or sector data with source and date.

Check: Explain how a trend can help one company and hurt another.

Lesson 18Ping An

How can government rules affect future profit?

Focus: regulation risk.

  • regulation - government rules that affect how a company operates.
  • compliance cost - the cost of obeying rules and standards.
  • policy risk - the risk that rule changes reduce future profit.

Formula: no new formula; classify possible rule changes by effect.

Evidence: Ping An report extract or regulatory note with a careful non-political framing.

Check: Explain why a profitable company may still face regulation risk.

Lesson 19Samsung

Why can exchange rates matter to shareholders?

Focus: global sales, currencies and reporting effects.

  • exchange rate - the price of one currency in terms of another.
  • export exposure - reliance on sales to customers in other countries.
  • translation effect - how currency changes affect reported figures.

Formula: exchange-rate change % = (new rate - old rate) / old rate x 100.

Evidence: Samsung geographic sales or currency-risk note with a frozen exchange-rate example.

Check: Calculate an exchange-rate change and link it to investor risk.

Lesson 20ChinaAMC CSI 300 ETF

How can one fund hold many companies?

Focus: China-relevant ETF/index-fund diversification compared with owning one company share.

  • ETF - an exchange-traded fund that can be bought and sold on a stock exchange.
  • index fund - a fund designed to track a market index rather than pick individual shares.
  • fund manager - the organisation responsible for running the fund.
  • diversification - spreading investment across different holdings to reduce risk.
  • expense ratio - annual fund cost as a percentage of invested assets.
  • tracking - how closely a fund follows its target index.

Formula: portfolio weight = holding value / total portfolio value x 100; expense ratio = annual fund cost / fund assets x 100 if data is suitable.

Evidence: ChinaAMC CSI 300 ETF or similar Mainland/HK fund factsheet, with BlackRock/iShares used only as a global comparison.

Check: Compare one ETF with one single-company share and explain which risk is reduced and which risks remain.

Lesson 21Tencent

What makes a platform company attractive or risky?

Focus: platform businesses and balanced judgement.

  • platform business - a business that connects different user groups.
  • network effects - when a service becomes more valuable as more people use it.
  • regulatory overhang - uncertainty caused by possible future rules.

Formula: combine growth, margin, P/E and risk notes; no single new formula.

Evidence: Tencent revenue mix, margin evidence and one risk note.

Check: Build a balanced judgement with one attraction and one risk.

Lesson 22BYD

Is BYD attractive because it is strong, or because the price is fair?

Focus: manufacturing strength and valuation discipline.

  • vertical integration - control over several stages of the supply chain.
  • capacity - how much a company can produce.
  • cyclicality - sensitivity to economic cycles and demand changes.

Formula: compare margin and growth; then add price and risk evidence.

Evidence: BYD sales, margin, production or valuation evidence with source date.

Check: Separate business strength from investment attractiveness.

Lesson 23HSBC

Why is analysing a bank different from analysing a tech company?

Focus: financial-company evidence and bank-specific risks.

  • interest margin - the spread between lending income and funding cost.
  • credit risk - the risk that borrowers do not repay.
  • capital strength - ability to absorb losses and keep operating.

Formula: basic ratio reading only; avoid advanced bank formulas in Grade 9 decks.

Evidence: HSBC results summary and simple bank-risk extract.

Check: Explain one reason bank analysis needs different evidence.

Lesson 24Anta Sports

How much is a strong brand worth to investors?

Focus: consumer brands, inventory and pricing power.

  • brand equity - value created by a strong brand name and reputation.
  • inventory risk - risk that unsold goods reduce profit.
  • pricing power - ability to raise prices without losing too many customers.

Formula: compare revenue growth and margin; no new formula.

Evidence: Anta Sports revenue, margin and brand or inventory notes.

Check: Link brand strength to profit, then add one limitation.

Lesson 25Nike

How can we compare a global brand with a China/HK brand?

Focus: comparable-company analysis across markets.

  • multinational - a company operating across several countries.
  • comparable company - a similar firm used for comparison.
  • local/global risk - risk from specific local markets or worldwide exposure.

Formula: compare two companies using the same metric set.

Evidence: Nike figures plus one China/HK brand comparison using aligned metrics.

Check: Write a fair comparison using the same metric for both companies.

Lesson 26Haidilao

Can a restaurant company recover after a difficult period?

Focus: turnaround evidence and store-network quality.

  • turnaround - improvement after a weak period.
  • store network - the set of locations through which a company serves customers.
  • same-store sales - sales from stores open in both comparison periods.

Formula: revenue per store = total revenue / number of stores, if data allows.

Evidence: Haidilao store count, revenue or operating recovery evidence.

Check: Decide whether one recovery figure is enough evidence.

Lesson 27Trip.com

Why can travel companies rise and fall with the economy?

Focus: cyclical demand and operating leverage.

  • cyclical demand - demand that rises and falls with economic conditions.
  • recovery - return toward earlier activity or profit levels.
  • operating leverage - profit sensitivity when fixed costs are high.

Formula: operating margin = operating profit / revenue x 100.

Evidence: Trip.com revenue and operating profit before and after a travel cycle change.

Check: Explain why higher travel demand may have a larger effect on profit.

Lesson 28Kuaishou

How does a free app turn users into revenue?

Focus: monetisation and user metrics.

  • active users - people who use a service during a measured period.
  • monetisation - turning user activity into revenue.
  • ARPU - average revenue per user.

Formula: ARPU = revenue / active users.

Evidence: Kuaishou user number and revenue data from one reporting period.

Check: Explain why more users do not automatically mean higher ARPU.

Lesson 29Lenovo

When does borrowing become a risk for shareholders?

Focus: debt, liabilities and leverage risk.

  • debt - money a company has borrowed and must repay.
  • liabilities - obligations a company owes to others.
  • gearing or leverage - the use of debt in a company's financing.

Formula: debt-to-equity = total debt / equity, if the data is suitable.

Evidence: Lenovo balance-sheet extract with debt, liabilities or financing notes.

Check: Explain one benefit and one risk of borrowing.

Lesson 30Costco

Can a steady business still be a bad investment at the wrong price?

Focus: quality, defensiveness and final valuation synthesis.

  • defensive business - a business whose demand may be more stable in difficult times.
  • quality company - a company with strong operations, brand, margins or resilience.
  • valuation risk - risk that the price already assumes too much good news.

Formula: synthesis of growth, margin, P/E and risk; no new formula.

Evidence: Costco growth, margin and valuation evidence from dated sources.

Check: Write a balanced final judgement using evidence, price and risk.

Course standards

How lessons are built

These rules keep the lessons focused, fair and evidence-based as new decks, quizzes and data snapshots are added.

Lesson focus

One clear decision

Each lesson starts with the company and analyst question, then builds toward one evidence task, one knowledge target and one short written judgement.

Evidence rules

Use dated evidence

Every data point should show the company, stock code or listing, source title, source URL, publication date, accessed date, key figures and what the evidence can and cannot prove.

Language support

Key terms get support

English stays as the main teaching language, with Chinese support for key terms, objectives, difficult prompts and formula wording.

Practice checks

Check knowledge before judgement

You practise definitions, quick calculations, quote-page reading, evidence selection, risk notes and short explain/evaluate answers.

Short team tasks

Finish together in one class

Use quick collaboration for scenario sorting, quote-page bid/ask labs, ETF vs single-stock comparisons and company-risk debates. Each task ends with an individual written check.
