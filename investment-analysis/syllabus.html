Grade 9 Investment Analysis

Investment Analysis: Evidence-Based Investing

The standard 50-lesson route teaches students to move from first opinion to evidence-based judgement through company, fund, bond, portfolio and investor-fit dilemmas across two semesters.

How the course works
Real cases Lessons use companies, funds, bonds, profiles and portfolio choices as case anchors.
Two semesters Semester 1 covers Lessons 1-30; Semester 2 covers Lessons 31-50.
Careful judgement Students finish with a practical action: consider, watch, avoid, compare with another choice or gather more evidence.
Student overview

Five units, fifty taught lessons

Each 40-minute lesson begins with a concrete investor dilemma students can answer before definitions. Semester 1 covers Lessons 1-30 with three checkpoints; Semester 2 covers Lessons 31-50 with two checkpoints.

Semester 1

Start with a real judgement

Lessons 1-30 build foundations, market access, company evidence, financial statements, returns, valuation and investor fit. Exams follow Lessons 10, 20 and 30.

Semester 2

Broaden the investment decision

Lessons 31-50 add deposits, bonds, funds, fees, diversification, behaviour, monitoring, comparison and final memo writing. Exams follow Lessons 40 and 50.

Lessons 1-10

Foundations, assets and market access

Students compare saving, investing, speculation, asset classes, shares, exchanges, quote pages, evidence logs, bonds, funds and first portfolio judgement.

Lessons 11-20

Company evidence and financial statements

Students use revenue, margins, cash flow, balance sheets, debt, dividends, risk registers and peer comparison to judge company evidence.

Lessons 21-30

Returns, valuation and investor fit

Students connect return, market value, P/E, valuation risk, dividends, diversification, investor profiles, currency risk and a first-semester memo.

Lessons 31-40

Funds, bonds, costs, diversification and behaviour

Students broaden beyond shares with deposits, bond yields, credit risk, index funds, active funds, ETF concentration, geography, gradual investing and plan rules.

Lessons 41-50

Portfolio decision process, monitoring and final memo

Students compare products, build starter portfolios, monitor holdings, rebalance, control costs, avoid performance chasing and write a final evidence-based memo.

Teaching model

Decision-first evidence-based investing

The syllabus treats topics, formulas and definitions as tools for improving a student judgement. Every lesson has a case anchor, starter dilemma, first judgement, missing evidence, key idea, practice task, misconception check and exit judgement in the canonical course map.

Simpler lesson structure

Hook, key idea, try it, decide

Every lesson should feel like one clear investigation. Students make a first judgement, learn one useful idea because that judgement needs improvement, use the idea on evidence, then make a careful next-action judgement.

How to invest

A practical investor workflow

Students use this same workflow across the course. It turns evidence gathering into a repeatable investing process without relying on tips, live prices or guessing short-term market moves.

Assessment checkpoints

Five exams across the year

Exams are separate checkpoints after each ten-lesson unit. Semester 1 has exams after Lessons 10, 20 and 30; Semester 2 has exams after Lessons 40 and 50.

Exam 2: after Lessons 11-20

Course standards

How lessons are built

These rules keep the lessons focused, fair and evidence-based as new decks, quizzes and data snapshots are added.

Lesson focus

One clear decision

Each lesson starts with the case anchor and investor question, retrieves prior knowledge, adds a focused knowledge target, then builds toward Evidence and Data Analysis and one short written judgement.

Evidence rules

Use dated evidence

Every data point should show the case anchor, product or security identifier, source title, source URL, publication date, accessed date, key figures and what the evidence can and cannot prove.

Handout book

Textbook equals handouts

Each lesson handout is the written lesson product. The textbook is the compiled sequence of those handouts with light front matter and unit dividers only.

Language support

Key terms get support

English stays as the main teaching language, with Chinese support for key terms, objectives, difficult prompts and formula wording.

Practice checks

Check knowledge before judgement

You practise definitions, yes/no checks, multiple-choice checks, matching or classification, quick calculations, evidence selection, analyse-why chains and short written judgements.

Short team tasks

Finish together in one class

Use quick collaboration for scenario sorting, quote-page bid/ask labs, ETF vs single-stock comparisons and company-risk debates. Each task ends with an individual written check.
