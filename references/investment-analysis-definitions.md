# Investment Analysis: Company Analysis

**Textbook-style Definition Overview**

This file is the shared definition source for the Investment Analysis company-analysis course. It expands the shorter slide vocabulary into complete textbook-style definitions for lesson planning, handouts, textbook chapters, quizzes and assessment design.

Priority source rule: where a course term overlaps with the CFA Program glossary, use the CFA-aligned investment and accounting meaning first, then simplify the wording for Grade 9 classroom use. Where no clear CFA glossary equivalent exists, use the course-specific definition from the company-analysis map. Clear CFA glossary matches are recorded in `references/investment-analysis-cfa-glossary-matches.json`; use the official CFA glossary link there for the original source wording.

Each row contains the course reference, English term, Chinese support, complete definition and the main course use. Slide wording may be shortened for display, but handouts and textbook-style materials should preserve the meaning here.

## Unit 1: Market foundations

| Ref | Term | Chinese support | Textbook definition | Course use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U1 L1 | investment analysis | 投资分析 | Investment analysis is the process of using source-dated evidence about an asset, business, return, risk and price before making a justified investment judgement. | Sets the evidence-before-judgement rule for the whole course. |
| U1 L1 | investment | 投资 | Investment is the act of putting money into an asset with the aim of earning a future return, such as income or capital gain, while accepting the possibility of loss. | Separates careful long-run judgement from guessing. |
| U1 L1 | speculation | 投机 | Speculation is an attempt to profit from uncertain price movements, usually relying more on expectations, timing or risk-taking than on complete evidence. | Builds the course boundary against short-term tips and unsupported guesses. |
| U1 L1 | asset | 资产 | An asset is something with economic value that can be owned or controlled, such as cash, property, a bond, a share or a business resource. | Gives students the basic category before they compare asset risk. |
| U1 L1 | share | 股票 / 股份 | A share is one unit of ownership in a company, giving the shareholder a claim on part of the company's equity and, depending on the share class, certain rights such as votes or dividends. | Defines what students are analysing when they discuss listed companies. |
| U1 L2 | stock exchange | 证券交易所 | A stock exchange is a regulated trading venue where investment instruments such as listed shares, ETFs and bonds can be bought and sold under market rules. | Explains why shares need organised market infrastructure. |
| U1 L2 | listing | 上市 | A listing is the formal permission for a company's securities to trade on a stock exchange after the company meets the exchange's requirements. | Connects companies, exchanges and tradable shares. |
| U1 L2 | stock code | 股票代码 | A stock code is the short market identifier used to find a listed security on an exchange or market-data system. | Helps students identify the exact security being analysed. |
| U1 L2 | liquidity | 流动性 | Liquidity is the ability to trade an asset quickly, at relatively low cost and in meaningful quantities without causing a large price change. | Introduces trading friction and why active markets matter. |
| U1 L3 | quote page | 报价页面 | A quote page is a market-data snapshot showing information such as the latest price, price movement, bid, ask, volume and security identifier for a listed asset. | Teaches students to read price evidence without treating it as a full judgement. |
| U1 L3 | volume | 成交量 | Volume is the number of shares or other securities traded during a stated period, such as a trading day. | Shows how much trading activity sits behind a price movement. |
| U1 L3 | bid | 买价 | The bid is the price at which a buyer, dealer or trader is currently willing to buy a specified quantity of a security. | Introduces one side of the market quote. |
| U1 L3 | ask | 卖价 | The ask is the price at which a seller, dealer or trader is currently willing to sell an asset, usually for a specified maximum quantity. | Introduces the other side of the market quote. |
| U1 L4 | voting rights | 投票权 | Voting rights are shareholder rights to vote on certain company decisions, such as electing directors or approving major corporate actions. | Clarifies that ownership can include decision rights. |
| U1 L4 | ordinary share | 普通股 | An ordinary share is a standard equity share in a company that usually carries voting rights and may receive dividends after higher-priority claims are considered. | Distinguishes normal shareholder ownership from other claims. |
| U1 L4 | control | 控制权 | Control is the ability to influence or decide company actions, usually through ownership, voting power, board influence or management authority. | Prevents students from thinking one small share controls a company. |
| U1 L5 | evidence log | 证据记录 | An evidence log is a structured record of sources, dates, figures, notes and limitations used to support an investment judgement. | Makes source discipline visible and repeatable. |
| U1 L5 | source date | 来源日期 | A source date is the date on which evidence was published, reported or measured. | Helps students judge how current evidence is. |
| U1 L5 | accessed date | 访问日期 | An accessed date is the date on which the analyst viewed or used a source. | Records when changing online evidence was checked. |

## Unit 2: Financial statements

| Ref | Term | Chinese support | Textbook definition | Course use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U2 L6 | revenue | 收入 | Revenue is the amount charged or earned from delivering goods or services in the ordinary activities of a business over a stated period, before expenses are deducted. | Starts financial-statement analysis with the top line. |
| U2 L6 | sales growth | 销售增长 | Sales growth is the increase in a company's revenue over time, usually measured as a percentage change between two periods. | Lets students compare whether demand or business activity is expanding. |
| U2 L6 | business model | 商业模式 | A business model is the way a company creates value, earns revenue, pays costs and turns customer demand into profit or cash flow. | Links revenue evidence to how the company actually works. |
| U2 L7 | cost of sales | 销售成本 | Cost of sales is the cost directly connected with the goods or services sold in a period; for inventory businesses, it is closely related to cost of goods sold. | Shows why sales alone do not prove strength. |
| U2 L7 | gross profit | 毛利 | Gross profit is revenue or sales minus cost of sales, before operating expenses, financing costs and taxes are deducted. | Measures profit before operating expenses and financing items. |
| U2 L7 | gross margin | 毛利率 | Gross margin is gross profit divided by revenue and expressed as a percentage, showing how much of each unit of sales remains after direct costs. | Gives students their first profitability percentage. |
| U2 L8 | operating profit | 营业利润 | Operating profit is profit from a company's usual business activities before taxes and before some financing effects are deducted; it is also called operating income. | Focuses attention on the core business rather than one-off items. |
| U2 L8 | operating margin | 营业利润率 | Operating margin is a profitability ratio calculated as operating income or operating profit divided by revenue. | Helps students compare efficiency across companies or years. |
| U2 L8 | percentage-point difference | 百分点差异 | A percentage-point difference is the arithmetic difference between two percentages, not a percentage change. | Prevents mistakes when comparing margins. |
| U2 L9 | operating cash flow | 经营现金流 | Operating cash flow is the net amount of cash provided by or used in a company's operating activities during a period. | Separates cash generation from accounting profit. |
| U2 L9 | capital expenditure | 资本支出 | Capital expenditure is spending on physical or long-term fixed assets such as property, factories, equipment, technology infrastructure or stores. | Explains why growing companies may need cash. |
| U2 L9 | free cash flow | 自由现金流 | Free cash flow is cash available to a company's investors after the company has made the investments needed to maintain itself as an ongoing business. | Introduces cash available after reinvestment needs. |
| U2 L10 | benchmark | 基准 | A benchmark is a standard or point of reference used to evaluate investment, portfolio or company performance. | Gives students a disciplined comparison point. |
| U2 L10 | trend | 趋势 | A trend is a longer-term pattern of movement in a particular direction, such as rising, falling or remaining broadly stable. | Moves analysis beyond one-year snapshots. |
| U2 L10 | scale | 规模 | Scale is the size of a company or activity, measured by figures such as revenue, users, stores, assets or market value. | Helps students compare large and small companies fairly. |

## Unit 3: Returns and valuation

| Ref | Term | Chinese support | Textbook definition | Course use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U3 L11 | capital gain | 资本利得 | A capital gain is the profit made when an asset is sold for more than its purchase price. | Defines one way shareholders can make money. |
| U3 L11 | capital loss | 资本损失 | A capital loss is the loss made when an asset is sold for less than its purchase price. | Defines how price movement can hurt investors. |
| U3 L11 | return | 回报 | Return is the gain or loss earned from an investment over a stated holding period, including price change and any income received. | Creates the basic measure of investment outcome. |
| U3 L12 | dividend | 股息 | A dividend is a distribution paid to shareholders based on the number of shares they own, usually in cash or additional shares. | Defines the income part of shareholder return. |
| U3 L12 | dividend yield | 股息收益率 | Dividend yield is the annual dividend per share divided by the share price and expressed as a percentage. | Lets students compare income relative to price. |
| U3 L12 | total return | 总回报 | Total return measures price appreciation or loss plus any income received from the investment over the period. | Prevents students from looking only at price movement. |
| U3 L13 | market capitalisation | 市值 | Market capitalisation is the total market value of a company's equity, calculated as share price multiplied by shares outstanding. | Shows how the market values the whole listed company. |
| U3 L13 | shares outstanding | 已发行股数 | Shares outstanding are the shares issued by a company and currently held by investors. | Supplies the second part of market-cap calculation. |
| U3 L13 | mega-cap | 超大市值公司 | A mega-cap company is a listed company with a very large market capitalisation compared with most companies in the market. | Gives students a size category for major listed firms. |
| U3 L14 | EPS | 每股收益 | EPS, or earnings per share, is the income earned during a period for each ordinary share, usually calculated as profit attributable to ordinary shareholders divided by the share count used in the calculation. | Connects profit to one share. |
| U3 L14 | P/E ratio | 市盈率 | The P/E ratio is the ratio of share price to earnings per share, showing how much investors pay for each unit of current earnings. | Introduces a simple valuation multiple. |
| U3 L14 | valuation | 估值 | Valuation is the process of estimating the value of an asset using variables related to future investment returns or comparisons with similar assets. | Turns price into an evidence-based judgement. |
| U3 L15 | cheap or expensive | 便宜或昂贵 | A share is cheap or expensive only in relation to evidence about value, earnings, growth, risk and expectations, not simply because the price per share is low or high. | Breaks the low-price-equals-cheap misconception. |
| U3 L15 | expectations | 预期 | Expectations are assumptions about future performance, risk or growth that investors may already have reflected in the current price. | Helps explain why good news can already be priced in. |
| U3 L15 | margin of safety | 安全边际 | Margin of safety is the room for error between the price paid and a cautious estimate of value. | Gives students a risk-control idea for valuation. |

## Unit 4: Risk, portfolios and funds

| Ref | Term | Chinese support | Textbook definition | Course use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U4 L16 | company-specific risk | 公司特定风险 | Company-specific risk is non-systematic risk tied to a particular company's operations, reputation, financial position, management or business environment. | Focuses students on risks that affect one company. |
| U4 L16 | demand risk | 需求风险 | Demand risk is the risk that customers buy less than expected, reducing revenue, profit or growth. | Links customer behaviour to future business results. |
| U4 L16 | execution risk | 执行风险 | Execution risk is the risk that a company fails to carry out its strategy, project or operational plan successfully. | Shows why plans are not the same as results. |
| U4 L17 | sector | 行业 | A sector is a group of related industries or companies that share similar business activities or economic exposures. | Creates a wider comparison group for company analysis. |
| U4 L17 | competition | 竞争 | Competition is rivalry between firms for customers, revenue, market share, talent, suppliers or profit. | Explains why strong companies can still face pressure. |
| U4 L17 | consumer trend | 消费趋势 | A consumer trend is a change in what customers prefer, buy or avoid over time. | Links changing tastes to company risk. |
| U4 L18 | regulation | 监管 | Regulation is a system of government rules and enforcement that affects how companies, markets or investors may behave. | Introduces rules as a source of risk and discipline. |
| U4 L18 | compliance cost | 合规成本 | Compliance cost is the money, time and resources a company spends to obey laws, rules, standards and reporting requirements. | Shows how regulation can reduce profit or change operations. |
| U4 L18 | policy risk | 政策风险 | Policy risk is the risk that changes in laws, regulation, taxation, subsidies or government priorities reduce future profit or change business prospects. | Helps students analyse government-related uncertainty. |
| U4 L19 | exchange rate | 汇率 | An exchange rate is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another currency. | Gives the currency basis for global company analysis. |
| U4 L19 | export exposure | 出口敞口 | Export exposure is a company's reliance on sales to customers in other countries or currencies. | Links revenue geography to currency and demand risk. |
| U4 L19 | translation effect | 折算影响 | A translation effect is the change in reported financial figures caused by converting foreign-currency results into the company's reporting currency. | Explains why accounts can move when currencies move. |
| U4 L20 | ETF | 交易型开放式指数基金 | An ETF is an exchange-traded fund, a pooled investment fund that holds a basket of assets and trades on a stock exchange like a share. | Introduces funds as an alternative to single shares. |
| U4 L20 | index fund | 指数基金 | An index fund is a fund designed to track the performance of a market index rather than choose individual securities through active stock-picking. | Shows passive diversification. |
| U4 L20 | diversification | 分散化 | Diversification is spreading investment across different holdings, sectors, regions or asset types to reduce dependence on any single source of risk. | Teaches the main reason one fund can hold many companies. |
| U4 L20 | portfolio weight | 投资组合权重 | Portfolio weight is the value of one holding divided by the total value of the portfolio and expressed as a percentage. | Measures concentration in one holding. |

## Unit 5: Sector case labs

| Ref | Term | Chinese support | Textbook definition | Course use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U5 L21 | platform business | 平台型业务 | A platform business connects different user groups and creates value by enabling interactions, transactions, content, services or data exchange between them. | Explains why user networks matter for some companies. |
| U5 L21 | network effects | 网络效应 | Network effects occur when a product or service becomes more valuable as more users, buyers, sellers or partners join it. | Defines a key strength and risk in platform cases. |
| U5 L21 | regulatory overhang | 监管不确定性 | Regulatory overhang is uncertainty created when possible future regulation may affect a company's operations, valuation or investor confidence. | Links policy uncertainty to valuation. |
| U5 L22 | vertical integration | 垂直整合 | Vertical integration is a strategy in which a company controls more than one stage of its supply chain, such as production, components, distribution or retail. | Helps analyse manufacturing and supply-chain advantage. |
| U5 L22 | capacity | 产能 | Capacity is the maximum amount a company can produce, deliver or serve using its available resources over a period. | Connects expansion plans to production limits. |
| U5 L22 | cyclicality | 周期性 | Cyclicality is the sensitivity of a company's sales, profit or valuation to changes in the economic cycle or industry demand cycle. | Shows why some businesses rise and fall more sharply. |
| U5 L23 | interest margin | 利差 | Interest margin is the spread between interest earned on loans or other earning assets and interest paid on deposits or other funding; net interest margin compares that spread with average earning assets. | Gives students a bank-specific profit driver. |
| U5 L23 | credit risk | 信用风险 | Credit risk is the risk of loss caused by a borrower or counterparty failing to make timely payments or by a change in the value of a financial instrument because default risk changes. | Explains why bank assets can become losses. |
| U5 L23 | capital strength | 资本实力 | Capital strength is a financial institution's ability to absorb losses and continue operating, supported by equity capital, reserves and regulatory capital ratios. | Helps students judge bank resilience. |
| U5 L24 | brand equity | 品牌权益 | Brand equity is the value created by a company's brand name, reputation, customer loyalty and perceived quality. | Connects non-physical assets to pricing and demand. |
| U5 L24 | inventory risk | 库存风险 | Inventory risk is the risk that unsold or obsolete goods reduce profit through discounting, write-downs, storage costs or lost working capital. | Links product demand to financial pressure. |
| U5 L24 | pricing power | 定价能力 | Pricing power is the ability to raise prices or maintain high prices without losing too many customers or too much sales volume. | Explains a possible advantage of strong brands. |
| U5 L25 | multinational | 跨国公司 | A multinational is a company that operates, sells, produces or earns revenue in more than one country. | Supports local-versus-global comparison. |
| U5 L25 | comparable company | 可比公司 | A comparable company is a firm with similar business risk, preferably in the same industry, used as a reference point for comparing growth, margins, valuation or risk. | Gives students a disciplined peer comparison tool. |
| U5 L25 | local/global risk | 本地/全球风险 | Local/global risk is the contrast between risks tied to a specific local market and risks created by worldwide exposure across countries, currencies and regulations. | Frames comparison between domestic and global brands. |

## Unit 6: Synthesis case labs

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U6 L26 | turnaround | 经营复苏 | A turnaround is an improvement in a company's performance after a weak period, often involving recovery in sales, profit, cash flow, operations or confidence. | Helps students analyse recovery cases cautiously. |

| U6 L29 | liabilities | 负债 | Liabilities are present obligations arising from past events that are expected to require an outflow of economic resources; they are claims by creditors and others on a company's resources. | Broadens analysis beyond formal debt. |
| U6 L29 | gearing or leverage | 负债杠杆 | Gearing or leverage is the use of fixed financing costs, especially debt and interest expense, in a company's capital structure; it can increase both potential shareholder returns and financial risk. | Helps students judge when borrowing becomes dangerous. |
| U6 L30 | defensive business | 防御型业务 | A defensive business is a business whose demand, revenue or profit may be more stable than average during difficult economic conditions. | Gives students a quality-and-risk category for final synthesis. |
| U6 L30 | quality company | 优质公司 | A quality company is a company with strong operations, durable advantages, resilient demand, sound finances, good margins or reliable cash generation. | Helps students avoid judging only by price. |
| U6 L30 | valuation risk | 估值风险 | Valuation risk is the risk that investment returns disappoint because the price already assumes too much good news or is high relative to evidence. | Completes the quality-price-risk judgement. |
